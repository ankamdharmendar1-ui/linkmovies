The Evolution of Aspect Ratios: Why Movies Keep Changing Shapes

July 24, 2026 Camera & Cinematography

Framing the Story

When you sit down in a theater or turn on your TV, you might notice black bars on the top and bottom of the screen, or on the sides. This is due to the aspect ratio—the proportional relationship between the width and height of the image. But why isn't there just one standard size?

The Classic 4:3 (Academy Ratio)

In the early days of cinema and television, screens were nearly square. The standard Academy Ratio (1.37:1) was used for classics like Casablanca and The Wizard of Oz. Today, some modern directors, like Wes Anderson (The Grand Budapest Hotel) or Zack Snyder (Justice League), revert to this boxy ratio to give their films a vintage, portrait-like intimacy.

The Widescreen War

When television started stealing audiences in the 1950s, Hollywood panicked. Their solution? Give audiences an experience they couldn't get in their living rooms: Widescreen. Technologies like CinemaScope introduced ultra-wide aspect ratios (like 2.35:1 or 2.39:1). This allowed for sweeping, epic landscapes—perfect for Westerns and massive sci-fi battles.

The Modern 16:9 Standard

Eventually, TV manufacturers compromised and created the 16:9 (1.78:1) standard for HDTVs, which is a middle ground between the old square TVs and ultra-wide cinema screens. However, many films are still shot in 2.39:1, which is why you still see black bars on your modern TV.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why do some IMAX movies change aspect ratios halfway through?
A: Directors like Christopher Nolan shoot specific action sequences on IMAX cameras (which have a taller 1.43:1 ratio) to immerse the audience, but use standard widescreen for dialogue scenes where IMAX cameras are too loud to record audio.
